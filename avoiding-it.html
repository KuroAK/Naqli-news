Fake news - How to avoid it

#1 CRAAP

Use the CRAAP test to scrutinise the legitimacy of a site.

- Currency : is the information you see on the site of current time? With facebook for instance, a story could be dated back a few months or even years and yet your friends are outraged as though it was a recent post.

- Relevance: Is the information you see of any use to what you're researching? This component of the test is most suited to those who find themselves actively seeking out the information as oppose to merely stumbling upon it. Is the information targeted to the level you want (elementary/advanced)? And is it related to the questions your researching for? Have you taken steps to look into various other sources before sticking with this particular one?

- Authority: who is the author of the article? Are they an expert on the subject they speak about within said article? Do they have an ulterior motive?

- Accuracy: Is the information you see backed by evidence? Does the author make mention of sources that are credible?

Purpose: What is the articles purpose? To cause heated outrage? To inform of something? To sell something? This question will end up leaving you with clues to whether bias exists in the article.

#2: Google is your best tool

If you find yourself drawn to an article on social media, before you go any further take 5 seconds to search it on Google. Most likely, a google search tells you:

  1. If another credible news site is reporting on the exact same subject as your article.
  2. If a fact-check website has deemed the article as false.
  3. If the majority of news sites reporting the subject of your article all hold biased views -- if this proves to be true then more research is required.

In the end taking 5 seconds to load up a google search will save you from clicking the like or share button on what was in fact fake news leading you to nothing but guilt and embarassment.

#3 Get your news from actual sources

Don't fall for the easiest trap of fake news, never get your news from social media feeds that includes the trending tab on the likes of Facebook and Twitter.

Instead, Head straight to credible sources... The ones that shell out newspapers especially:

  • The times
  • Metro
  • London evening standard

By no means does this exclude more modern news outlets that have a proven track record of legitimacy, for example:

  • BBC news
  • Sky news
  • ITV news

#4 Don't blindly trust opinions over the facts

Nowadays, all news outlets have a dedicated space in their newspapers for people's opinions of a story (article). This space is usually titled something like:

  • Editorials
  • Letters to the editor
  • Opinion
  • Op-Eds

You may find yourself taking on the opinions presented as yourself, using them as a way to fully understand the facts without necessarily looking at the actual facts being opinionised. This is where the trap lies you're taking opinions as fact.

To prevent this you must realise that they speak of the facts in a way that adheres to their feeling on the contents of the story then all there's left to do after that is to think for yourself 'how would the opposing side argue against the opinions?" To find the answer simply read the story associated with these opinions.

#5 Beware the red flags!

  • Check the url (link) does it end in a .co as oppose to a .com?
  • Are there any disclaimers however small, perhaps something that says "satire" (the stories are just for jokes)?
  • When you click the article in social media, does it go to something that is outdated? Why would something outdated be circulated nowadays?
  • Was the article in question posted by someone who has no real high knowledge on the article's subject? 'that one friend' for instance.
